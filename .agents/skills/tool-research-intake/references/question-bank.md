# Question Bank

Use these questions to create a usable research brief before broader analysis starts.

## Intake Rules

- Start with a lightweight orientation pass on the candidate tool and category.
- Use the orientation pass only to tailor the intake prompts. Do not let it become full research.
- Ask one question per turn.
- Plan a short intake sequence of 3 to 4 broad questions.
- Do not summarize, reason, or recommend until that intake sequence is complete.
- Do not send a grouped questionnaire in a single message.
- Do not ask about expected user count, seat count, budget range, or pricing ceilings during intake. If the user volunteers commercial context, capture it without reopening the intake sequence.
- Start with the core intake round below and only ask follow-ups for material gaps.
- If the user already answered a question indirectly, do not ask it again.
- If a detail is unknown, mark it as an assumption instead of drilling for more detail.

## Orientation Pass

Before the main intake, quickly identify:

- The tool category and primary job to be done
- The likely users and buyer motion
- The deployment or hosting model when obvious
- The likely comparison set or incumbent category
- The category-specific risks or requirements that may justify extra follow-up questions

Use fast, high-signal sources such as the vendor homepage, product docs overview, or public demo materials. Stop as soon as you can tailor the intake.

## Core Intake Round

Ask these questions one at a time. Wait until the end of this short sequence before summarizing or interpreting the answers.

- Scope and trigger:
  What tool are we evaluating, what problem or job should it solve, and why is this evaluation happening now?
- Decision and stakeholders:
  What decision are we trying to make, by when, who approves the choice, and what outcome would make this evaluation clearly successful or clearly not worth continuing?
- Users and current baseline:
  Who will use or be affected by this tool, what similar tools or workflows are in use today, and what is working well versus missing, slow, or frustrating?
- Constraints and evidence:
  What capabilities and integrations are mandatory, what non-negotiable constraints matter most, and what notes, documents, links, or prior analysis already exist?

## Baseline Follow-Up Questions

Ask these only when current tools already exist and the main intake sequence did not answer them well enough. Ask them one at a time, then summarize the baseline only after the follow-up sequence is complete.

- Are the current tools official standards or ad hoc choices?
- Are there contracts, sunk costs, migration barriers, or procurement constraints tied to them?
- Are there known user complaints, adoption issues, or role-specific pain points?
- Which parts of the workflow are already handled well enough today?
- What would have to be materially better to justify change?

## Optional Depth Follow-Up Questions

Use these when the evaluation needs more precision. Ask them one at a time and hold the summary until the end of the follow-up sequence.

- Which integrations or data flows are non-negotiable?
- Which metrics define success after adoption?
- Is this a team-level decision or an organization-wide platform decision?
- What would need to be true for a pilot to count as a real win?
- How much confidence is needed: a directional view, a shortlist, a pilot recommendation, or a full decision memo?
- Are there specific competitors that must be included even if they are not currently in use?
- Are there internal assumptions, political constraints, or prior decisions that could affect the recommendation?

## Category-Specific Follow-Up Examples

Use at most 1 to 2 of these when the orientation pass shows they matter. Ask them one at a time and fold the answers into the final intake summary.

- AI tools:
  Which models, data-handling rules, guardrails, or human-review requirements are non-negotiable?
- Design-to-code or prototyping tools:
  How important are code export quality, editability, design-system fidelity, and developer handoff?
- Developer infrastructure or platform tools:
  Are self-hosting, data residency, auditability, or production support requirements mandatory?
- Collaboration or workflow tools:
  Are admin controls, approval flows, cross-team adoption, or license governance major decision factors?

## Intake Output Format

Summarize the answers into a brief that includes:

- Scope of the evaluation
- Reason for evaluating now
- Decision owner and timeline
- Primary users and user-role assumptions
- Success criteria
- Current-state baseline candidates
- Decision criteria
- Constraints and migration barriers
- User-provided context and supporting material
- Known unknowns
